\abstract{Retinal foundation models can perform well on average while remaining unreliable for specific diseases, imaging modalities, or patient subgroups. We study this problem using a controlled evaluation of ophthalmic foundation-model outputs on Harvard-FairVision for age-related macular degeneration, diabetic retinopathy, and glaucoma, and on Harvard-GDP for glaucoma detection and progression forecasting. Across FairVision tasks, standalone models show clear differences in thresholded performance, calibration, and subgroup error rates. Static probability fusion and simple reliability routing do not remove these gaps. We therefore introduce Equi-Agent as a reliability-conditioned selective prediction framework that combines validation-derived model reliability priors, empirical-Bayes subgroup shrinkage, model disagreement, calibration uncertainty, and conformal escalation. On 9,000 FairVision held-out test cases, forced reliability-weighted arbitration produced F1 0.729 and balanced accuracy 0.807, comparable to static probability fusion, but selective escalation improved accepted-case F1 to 0.902 and worst-group accepted-case F1 to 0.615 while accepting 30.9\% of cases for automated decision. Metadata counterfactuals changed disease labels in only 0.4\% of 54,000 perturbations but changed escalation status in 5.8\%, supporting the intended use of demographics for reliability lookup rather than diagnosis. For GDP progression forecasting, Equi-Agent improves thresholded F1 from 0.600 to 0.684 and worst-group F1 from 0.564 to 0.619 while escalating 51 of 200 cases. These results support selective reliability auditing as a clinically relevant complement to retinal foundation-model prediction.}

\section{Introduction}
Retinal diseases such as age-related macular degeneration (AMD), diabetic retinopathy (DR), and glaucoma remain major causes of preventable visual impairment. Their diagnosis depends on subtle structural and functional evidence distributed across fundus photography, scanning laser ophthalmoscopy (SLO), optical coherence tomography (OCT), and visual-field testing. Automated systems are therefore attractive for scalable eye care, particularly in settings where specialist access is limited.

The recent emergence of ophthalmic foundation models has changed the technical landscape. Large pretrained retinal encoders can be adapted to multiple downstream tasks and often outperform smaller task-specific models. Yet average accuracy is an incomplete measure of clinical readiness. A model may perform well overall while being poorly calibrated for a subgroup, overcalling disease in one demographic stratum, or missing early pathology in another. This failure mode is especially consequential in ophthalmology, where anatomical baselines and disease appearance vary across age, ancestry, image modality, acquisition protocol, and disease stage.

Current mitigation strategies only partially address this problem. Static ensembles assume that model reliability can be averaged. Calibration methods rescale probabilities without deciding which evidence source should be trusted. Fairness-aware post-processing adjusts thresholds at the group level, but often treats subgroup membership as a fixed correction rather than as one component of a broader clinical context. In practice, the relevant question is more granular: given this patient, this image, this disease task, and this pattern of model disagreement, which output should influence the final decision, and with what degree of confidence?

Here we introduce Equi-Agent, a reliability-conditioned selective prediction framework for contextual arbitration among retinal foundation models. Equi-Agent treats model trust as dynamic rather than intrinsic. It integrates foundation-model predictions with patient metadata used only for reliability lookup, validation-derived subgroup reliability priors, model disagreement, conformal uncertainty, and a final safety audit. The framework is designed to produce not only a diagnostic label, but also a transparent account of model trust and an escalation recommendation when evidence is discordant or insufficient.

We make four contributions. First, we establish a controlled benchmark for comparing retinal foundation models under identical FairVision train, validation, and test splits with standardized subgroup and calibration metrics. Second, we formulate trust calibration as an explicit arbitration problem: model outputs are interpreted together with validation reliability priors, modality context, and safety checks rather than averaged blindly. Third, we introduce a deterministic reliability-conditioned selective arbitration layer with empirical-Bayes subgroup shrinkage, reliability-weighted model fusion, conformal escalation, and risk-coverage evaluation. Fourth, we evaluate metadata counterfactuals to test whether demographic context changes diagnosis directly or primarily changes confidence and escalation.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Foundation Models in Ophthalmic Imaging}
\label{subsec:related_foundation}
Foundation models for ophthalmic imaging learn reusable representations from large-scale OCT, fundus, SLO, or multimodal image-text corpora. RETFound represents OCT-oriented self-supervised pretraining, whereas MIRAGE, FLAIR, RET-CLIP, VisionFM, UrFound, and RetiZero reflect a broader family of retinal encoders intended for transfer across downstream tasks. These systems differ substantially in modality support, pretraining objective, image resolution, and downstream adaptation recipe, making uncontrolled comparison difficult.

We therefore evaluate foundation models under a shared transfer protocol wherever possible. OCT-oriented models are evaluated on OCT inputs, fundus/SLO-oriented models on SLO/fundus inputs, and all downstream thresholds are selected using validation data only. This protocol compares transferability under a common evaluation contract. It is not intended to be a claim that every foundation model has been tuned to its model-specific optimum.

\subsection{Bias and Fairness in Medical Vision Models}
Medical AI systems can reproduce or amplify disparities when training data, acquisition protocols, disease prevalence, or label quality differ across patient groups. In ophthalmology, these concerns include unequal sensitivity, unequal false-positive burden, and subgroup-specific calibration error. Such failures are not merely statistical; they can alter referral patterns, delay treatment, or increase unnecessary follow-up for already underserved populations.

Equi-Agent addresses fairness as a problem of reliability-aware inference. Rather than using demographic metadata as disease evidence, the framework uses validation-set behavior to estimate when a model's output is less trustworthy for a given subgroup and task. This distinction is central: demographic context informs trust in the model, not the biological likelihood of disease.

\subsection{Multi-Agent and LLM-Based Clinical Reasoning Systems}
Large language models have been used to summarize clinical records, retrieve guidelines, explain model outputs, and support multi-step medical reasoning. Multi-agent systems extend this idea by assigning specialized roles to separate reasoning modules. This can improve interpretability, but it also creates risks: hallucinated findings, prompt sensitivity, and poorly controlled decision logic.

Equi-Agent constrains language-model reasoning to evidence-grounded arbitration. Agents reason over supplied model probabilities, patient metadata, validation priors, image-derived findings, visual-field measurements, and guideline summaries. They are not used to infer hidden clinical measurements or replace the image models. The empirical question is whether this structured arbitration improves reliability beyond deterministic routing, static fusion, and post-hoc calibration.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{subsec:problem_formulation}
Let $x_i^{(m)}$ denote the imaging input for patient-eye case $i$ and modality $m$, and let $a_i$ denote available patient metadata, including age group, race, ethnicity, and sex/gender. For each disease task $t \in \{\mathrm{AMD}, \mathrm{DR}, \mathrm{glaucoma}\}$, a set of base models $\mathcal{M}$ produces probabilities $p_{ij}^{(t)} = f_j(x_i^{(m_j)})$ for model $j$. The objective is to estimate a final prediction $\hat{y}_i^{(t)}$ and confidence score that preserve aggregate diagnostic performance while reducing subgroup-specific reliability gaps.

Equi-Agent frames this as contextual arbitration. For a case $i$, the system estimates the trust assigned to model $j$ as a function of disease task, modality, patient metadata, subgroup-specific validation reliability, model confidence, and cross-model disagreement:
\begin{equation}
w_{ij}^{(t)} = g\left(t, m_j, a_i, p_{ij}^{(t)}, \mathcal{R}_{j,t,a_i}, \Delta_i^{(t)}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}_{j,t,a_i}$ denotes validation-derived reliability priors for the relevant subgroup context and $\Delta_i^{(t)}$ denotes disagreement among available model outputs. The final prediction is produced by an arbitration procedure that combines weighted model evidence with independent visual, functional, and safety assessments. This formulation separates two quantities that are often conflated: the probability of disease and the reliability of the model producing that probability.

\subsection{Datasets and Prediction Tasks}
\label{subsec:methods_datasets}
Harvard-FairVision is used for cross-sectional diagnosis of AMD, DR, and glaucoma. Each task is represented with fixed train, validation, and test splits, and each prediction row is linked to demographic metadata used for subgroup evaluation. Harvard-GDP is used for glaucoma detection and progression forecasting, including OCT B-scan, retinal nerve fiber layer thickness (RNFLT), visual-field, and clinical metadata fields where available. FairVision supports the primary cross-sectional fairness analysis, whereas Harvard-GDP tests whether arbitration can be extended to temporal disease progression.

\subsection{Base Vision Foundation Models}
\label{subsec:base_models_methods}
We evaluate modality-specific foundation-model outputs using a standardized prediction schema. RETFound is evaluated as an OCT model and MIRAGE as an SLO/fundus model. FLAIR, RET-CLIP, and RetiZero are evaluated as SLO/fundus image encoders with task-specific linear probes. VisionFM and UrFound are evaluated with both Fundus/SLO and OCT inputs. For FLAIR, RET-CLIP, RetiZero, VisionFM, and UrFound, image embeddings are extracted from the frozen pretrained model and a balanced logistic regression head is trained for each FairVision disease task. Thresholds are selected on the validation split using the prespecified validation metric for each run, and final metrics are computed only on the held-out test split.

This design separates representation evaluation from model-specific fine-tuning optimization. It allows controlled comparison under the same downstream split, label, threshold, and metric contract, while acknowledging that some foundation models may perform differently under native full fine-tuning or prompt-based protocols.

\subsection{Equi-Agent Architecture}
\label{subsec:architecture_methods}
Equi-Agent is organized as a structured arbitration system (Fig.~\ref{fig:equi_agent_architecture}). The BioProfiler converts structured metadata into a concise clinical narrative. The Vision Specialist processes OCT and SLO/fundus inputs, reports foundation-model probability distributions, and performs a morphology-grounded visual audit. The Functional Specialist interprets visual-field information, especially mean deviation (MD), to provide glaucoma-relevant functional context. The Equity Auditor compares the current patient context with validation-derived subgroup error priors and recommends whether sensitivity- or precision-oriented trust adjustment is warranted. The Guidelines Agent retrieves and summarizes external clinical guidance when available. The Orchestrator integrates these findings into final disease labels. The Safety Agent performs a final uncertainty and discordance audit and can recommend acceptance, override, escalation to human review, or insufficient-data status.

\subsection{Reliability Calibration}
\label{subsec:dynamic_trust_methods}
Reliability calibration uses validation-set behavior to estimate when each model is more or less reliable. For each base model, disease task, and subgroup attribute, we compute validation AUROC, F1-score, false-positive rate, false-negative rate, expected calibration error, and sample counts. Subgroup priors are marked unstable when positive or negative counts are too small. At inference time, the arbitration layer first checks stable intersectional priors, then single-attribute priors, then global model priors.

The Equity Auditor converts these priors into trust recommendations. A high subgroup false-negative rate motivates a sensitivity shift, lowering the effective diagnostic threshold or increasing uncertainty when model confidence is borderline. A high false-positive rate motivates a precision shift, raising the threshold or down-weighting overconfident positive predictions. These adjustments act on model trust and confidence; demographic metadata is not used as direct disease evidence.

\subsection{Reliability-Conditioned Selective Arbitration}
\label{subsec:selective_arbitration_methods}
For the deterministic FairVision arbitration experiment, we instantiate the reliability prior as a fixed weighted score rather than as free-form language-model reasoning. For model $j$, task $t$, and subgroup context $g$, local subgroup reliability is shrunk toward the global validation estimate:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_g = \frac{n_g}{n_g + k},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\tilde{R}_{j,t,g} = \lambda_g R_{j,t,g}^{local} + (1-\lambda_g)R_{j,t}^{global},
\end{equation}
where $n_g$ is the subgroup validation support and $k=50$ in the current experiments. The scalar reliability score is
\begin{equation}
s_{j,t,g} =
0.45\,\mathrm{AUROC}_{j,t,g}
+0.35\,\mathrm{F1}_{j,t,g}
-0.10\,\mathrm{ECE}_{j,t,g}
-0.07\,\mathrm{FNR}_{j,t,g}
-0.03\,\mathrm{FPR}_{j,t,g}.
\end{equation}
The false-negative penalty is larger than the false-positive penalty because the current setting is screening-oriented; however, these weights are explicit hyperparameters and can be changed for referral-burden or confirmatory settings.

For case $i$, the model-specific arbitration weight is
\begin{equation}
w_{ij}^{(t)} =
\frac{
\exp\left(\beta s_{j,t,g_i} - \gamma U_{ij} - \delta D_{ij}\right)
}{
\sum_{k \in \mathcal{M}}
\exp\left(\beta s_{k,t,g_i} - \gamma U_{ik} - \delta D_{ik}\right)
},
\end{equation}
where $U_{ij}=1-2|p_{ij}^{(t)}-0.5|$ is probability uncertainty and $D_{ij}$ indicates disagreement with the majority model vote. The final forced probability is
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}_{i}^{(t)} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{M}}w_{ij}^{(t)}p_{ij}^{(t)}.
\end{equation}
This forced probability is used for standard diagnostic metrics. Separately, a selective prediction layer determines whether the case should be accepted for automated decision or escalated.

Selective escalation is triggered by close-call probability, high model disagreement, low weighted reliability, or conformal ambiguity. For conformal escalation, the validation split is used to compute a nonconformity score
\begin{equation}
c_i = 1-\hat{p}_{i,y_i}^{(t)},
\end{equation}
and the $(1-\alpha)$ quantile is estimated globally and with subgroup shrinkage. At test time, labels whose nonconformity score is below the threshold form a prediction set. Cases with ambiguous or empty sets are escalated. We report both forced metrics on all cases and accepted-case metrics among non-escalated cases.

\subsection{Contextual Arbitration Procedure}
\label{subsec:arbitration_methods}
The arbitration procedure receives structured inputs: model probabilities, validation thresholds, subgroup priors, patient metadata, visual morphology summaries when available, functional summaries when available, and guideline context. The deterministic FairVision selective-arbitration experiment uses foundation-model probabilities, validation reliability priors, model disagreement, and metadata for reliability lookup; it does not use visual-field MD or other functional glaucoma proxies. For GDP progression, functional evidence is available and can constrain image-only predictions when visual-field findings are normal or discordant. For AMD, arbitration uses a pathology-signal representation derived from cumulative stage probabilities and inspects whether disease probability mass is concentrated in early, intermediate, or advanced stages. For DR, arbitration emphasizes vascular lesion evidence and cross-model confidence.

The Safety Agent then audits the decision for discordant structural and functional evidence, missing modalities, poor image quality, or unusually high uncertainty. This final stage is designed to prevent inappropriate reassurance and to identify cases that should be escalated rather than forced into a binary output.

\subsection{Longitudinal Progression Reasoning}
\label{subsec:longitudinal_methods}
For Harvard-GDP, longitudinal extension treats each visit sequence as a temporal evidence stream. The temporal agent compares current and prior OCT/RNFL evidence, visual-field change, and model confidence trajectories. A progression decision should be supported by clinically plausible temporal change rather than by a single noisy scan. GDP baselines use RNFLT, OCT B-scan features, clinical metadata, and their combinations, and provide the baseline reference point for longitudinal arbitration.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:metrics_methods}
Evaluation is implemented with a standardized prediction schema containing patient identifiers, task, model name, split, ground-truth label, predicted probability, thresholded prediction, and demographic metadata. For Equi-Agent comparisons, we emphasize thresholded diagnostic metrics, including F1-score, balanced accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, Brier score, negative log-likelihood, and expected calibration error. Ranking metrics such as AUROC are retained for standalone model diagnostics where probability ranking is directly interpretable. For agent outputs, AUROC is secondary because the agent is designed to arbitrate thresholded decisions and escalation behavior rather than learn a new continuous ranking function.

Selective prediction is evaluated with coverage, escalation rate, accepted-case F1, accepted-case sensitivity and specificity, accepted-case error rate, escalated-case disease prevalence, and worst-group accepted-case F1. Risk-coverage curves report diagnostic performance as the accepted fraction is reduced from 100\% toward more conservative operating points. Metadata counterfactual safety is evaluated by holding image-model outputs fixed while perturbing demographic fields used for reliability lookup and measuring disease-label flip rate and escalation-state flip rate.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
\label{subsec:statistical_methods}
Unless otherwise stated, metrics are computed on the held-out test split after thresholds are selected on validation data. Confidence intervals will be estimated by nonparametric patient-level bootstrapping in the final analysis. Paired comparisons will be made on shared test cases, with multiple subgroup comparisons controlled using the Benjamini--Hochberg procedure.


\section{Experimental Design}\label{sec:experiments}

The evaluation has two parts. First, we assess whether ophthalmic foundation models show task- and subgroup-dependent reliability differences on Harvard-FairVision. Second, we test whether the same arbitration framework can be applied to Harvard-GDP glaucoma progression forecasting. All reported test metrics use held-out test splits. Thresholds, calibration rules, and routing policies are selected using validation data or prespecified rules, not the test labels.

Demographic metadata is used only to identify which validation-derived reliability priors apply to a model output. It is not used as direct disease evidence. For Equi-Agent outputs, thresholded metrics and safety behavior are primary because the agent is an arbitration system rather than a standalone continuous risk-ranking model. AUROC is retained for base models and reported only secondarily for agent outputs when a final probability is emitted.

\subsection{FairVision Reliability-Conditioned Selective Arbitration}
\label{subsec:fairvision_selective_results}

Reliability-conditioned selective arbitration was evaluated on all 9,000 FairVision held-out test cases shared by the nine available model streams, with 3,000 validation cases used for reliability priors and conformal thresholds. The forced reliability-weighted prediction did not substantially outperform static probability fusion when every case was assigned a binary label (F1 0.729 vs. 0.731). Its main effect was selective triage: by escalating high-uncertainty cases, the full reliability-conformal system improved accepted-case F1 to 0.902, accepted-case sensitivity to 0.890, accepted-case specificity to 0.914, and worst-group accepted-case F1 to 0.615, while accepting 30.9\% of cases for automated decision (Table~\ref{tab:fairvision_selective_ablation}).

Metadata counterfactuals were evaluated by holding image-model outputs fixed while perturbing reliability-lookup demographic fields. Across 54,000 counterfactual perturbations, the final disease label changed in 0.4\% of cases, whereas escalation status changed in 5.8\%. This supports the intended design: demographic metadata mainly affects uncertainty and escalation through validation reliability lookup rather than acting as a direct diagnostic signal.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
The completed experiments show that ophthalmic model reliability is task- and modality-dependent. RETFound OCT is the strongest completed FairVision diagnostic baseline, but its subgroup gaps remain large. Static fusion and simple routing do not solve this problem; in several cases they reduce worst-group F1 or worsen calibration despite using the same model outputs. These results support a practical conclusion: model selection for ophthalmic decision support should consider subgroup reliability and calibration, not only average F1 or AUROC.

The FairVision selective-arbitration experiment clarifies the main contribution. Reliability weighting alone did not materially improve forced classification over static fusion. The gain appeared when the system was evaluated as a selective predictor: accepted-case F1 increased to 0.902 and worst-group accepted-case F1 increased to 0.615, at the cost of escalating 69.1\% of cases under the most conservative full setting. This is a clinically meaningful but different claim from full automation. Equi-Agent is most defensible as a triage layer that identifies cases safe enough for automated decision and sends uncertain cases to human review.

Equi-Agent addresses this as an auditing and arbitration problem. The system does not treat demographics as disease evidence. Instead, demographic fields are used to select validation-derived reliability priors that describe how a model has behaved for similar cases. Metadata counterfactuals support this distinction: disease labels changed in only 0.4\% of counterfactual perturbations, while escalation status changed in 5.8\%. The final output is a thresholded prediction plus a confidence statement and escalation flag. This design is intentionally constrained: agent reasoning selects a calibration policy, while threshold values and allowable output fields are prespecified.

The GDP experiments clarify an important boundary. Cross-sectional glaucoma detection can be evaluated with OCT foundation-model probes, but longitudinal progression forecasting is better served by the native GDP RNFLT+TDS workflow. This is not a weakness of foundation models in general; it reflects the fact that progression labels are tied to longitudinal structural and functional change. For progression, Equi-Agent should be evaluated primarily by thresholded performance, calibration, and safety behavior rather than by treating its final probability as an independent continuous ranking score.

\subsection{Fairness of Foundation-Model Comparison}
\label{subsec:discussion_fairness_protocol}
The evaluation is fair in the sense of controlled downstream comparison. All foundation models use the same FairVision splits, train only on the training split, select thresholds only on the validation split, and report metrics only on the held-out test split. They are evaluated using the modality they are designed to process, and all prediction outputs are converted to the same schema before metric computation.

This does not mean every model has been optimized to its maximum possible performance. Some models may benefit from model-specific preprocessing, full fine-tuning, prompt engineering, higher input resolution, or multimodal usage. The reported results should therefore be interpreted as controlled transfer comparisons, not as definitive leaderboards of the foundation models themselves.

\subsection{Clinical and Methodological Implications}
\label{subsec:discussion_implications}
Equi-Agent supports a deployment pattern in which foundation models are treated as evidence sources rather than autonomous diagnostic authorities. A high-confidence model output should be trusted only when it is consistent with morphology, functional context, and validation reliability for the relevant subgroup. Conversely, a borderline or discordant model output can trigger threshold adjustment, uncertainty inflation, or human review.

This approach may be especially valuable in ophthalmology because multimodal evidence is naturally complementary. OCT can reveal retinal layer pathology, SLO/fundus imaging can reveal vascular and optic-disc patterns, and visual fields can reveal functional loss. An arbitration framework can exploit this complementarity while explicitly monitoring when a modality or model is unreliable.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limitations}
Several limitations remain. First, subgroup estimates can be unstable when positive or negative counts are small, especially for intersectional groups; empirical-Bayes shrinkage reduces but does not eliminate this problem. Second, the current selective rule is very conservative and escalates all DR cases, so disease-specific operating thresholds require retuning. Third, demographic metadata introduces ethical and methodological risk if it is used incorrectly. Counterfactual tests are encouraging, but metadata-only, shuffled-metadata, and randomized-prior controls remain needed before deployment claims. Fourth, LLM-based arbitration can be sensitive to prompt wording and model version, requiring repeated-run and prompt-paraphrase analyses. Finally, confidence intervals, external validation, and qualitative morphology review are still needed before the manuscript is ready for a strong clinical submission.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Equi-Agent reframes retinal foundation-model deployment as a reliability-auditing and selective-prediction problem. FairVision experiments show that standalone foundation models and simple ensembles can have substantial subgroup error and calibration gaps. Reliability-conditioned selective arbitration improves accepted-case performance and worst-group accepted-case reliability by escalating uncertain cases, rather than by pretending every case can be automated safely. Harvard-GDP experiments show that progression forecasting requires native longitudinal evidence rather than generic OCT B-scan transfer alone. The current evidence supports a cautious role for Equi-Agent: an interpretable arbitration layer that reports model reliability, applies prespecified calibration policies, and escalates uncertain cases rather than hiding uncertainty behind a single average metric.
